Goal & Overview

An 8-week program to design and build an AI-powered school handbook assistant. Stage 1 teaches HCI design through real user research. Stage 2 teaches the technical skills to build and ship a working web app.

8-Week Workflow

Two stages, eight weeks. Design first, then build.

Stage 1 — HCI Design (Weeks 1–4)

Understand users, collect evidence, design and test a prototype.

Week 1 — Understand and Plan

Task 1: Needfinding
  • Study the Needfinding videos in class (3.3.1, 3.3.3, 3.3.4, 3.3.6, 3.3.8, 3.3.11, 3.3.12, 3.3.15, 3.3.16, 3.3.20)
  • Choose your primary user group
  • Investigate 2–3 existing solutions
  • Decide which needfinding methods to use
  • Define your research goals
  • Submit Week 1 report (JDF format via Overleaf)

Week 2 — Design, Collect, and Analyze

Task 2: Data Collection
  • Study the data collection and need representation videos in class (3.3.13, 3.3.17, 3.3.18, 3.3.25, 3.3.3)
  • Draft your survey or interview guide
  • Pilot and revise your questions
  • Conduct interviews, surveys, or focus groups
  • Analyze findings and identify user needs, pain points, trust concerns
  • Turn findings into initial requirements
  • Submit Week 2 report (JDF format via Overleaf)

Week 3 — Prototype and Evaluate

Tasks 3 & 4: Prototyping + Evaluation
  • Study the Design Alternatives, Prototyping, and Design Principles videos in class
  • Create a simple prototype (mockup, not code)
  • Prepare evaluation tasks
  • Test the prototype with users using think-aloud
  • Record usability and trust-related issues
  • Submit Week 3 report (JDF format via Overleaf)

Week 4 — Iterate and Reflect

Task 5: Iteration
  • Review the most important problems
  • Revise the design
  • Test the updated version again, if possible
  • Write a short reflection on what improved
  • Submit Week 4 report (JDF format via Overleaf)

Learn RAG and ChromaDB, build the web app, test with real users, and ship.

  • Learn what ChromaDB is and how vector search works

  • Run the starter code and ingest a sample handbook

  • Submit Week 5 report (JDF format via Overleaf)

  • Handle loading states, empty states, and "not found" responses

What to Learn

A. How to explore the problem space

  • Who the target users are
  • What handbook-related questions they commonly have
  • How they currently look for answers (school website, asking a teacher, searching online)
  • What difficulties or frustrations they experience
  • What makes them trust or distrust an AI-generated answer

B. Methods for needfinding

  • What methods can be used for needfinding
  • When to use interviews, surveys, or focus groups
  • What kind of information each method can help you gather
  • How to choose participants who match your target users

C. How to turn findings into requirements

  • How to identify patterns in user responses
  • How to represent user needs clearly
  • How to summarize pain points and trust concerns
  • How to translate findings into system requirements

What to Do

Step 1: Choose your primary users

Recruit a small group of target users (students, parents, or teachers). Focus on one primary group.

Interviews: 3–5 participants · Surveys: 10–20 respondents · Focus groups: 4–6 per session

Step 2: Investigate the current landscape

Explore how users currently find handbook information. Review the school website, FAQ pages, or chatbot tools. Note 2–3 existing solutions with strengths and weaknesses.

Step 3: Choose your needfinding methods

Decide which methods to use based on what you want to learn. Interviews for detailed experiences, surveys for broad patterns, focus groups for comparing views.

Step 4: Define your research goals

Clearly state what you want to learn before writing any questions. For example: what topics are hardest to find? What does "trust" mean to users? When do users give up?

Ethics: When involving real participants, explain the study purpose, get consent, keep responses anonymous, and make participation voluntary.
Output
  • Your target user group and why you chose them
  • A brief review of 2–3 existing solutions and their limitations
  • The needfinding methods you plan to use and why
  • Your main research goals

What to Learn

  • How to write clear survey or interview questions
  • How to avoid biased, vague, leading, or double-barreled questions
  • How to design questions that match your research goals
  • How to revise weak questions if the first version is unclear
  • How to represent user needs in a structured and communicable form
  • How to translate needs into clear, actionable requirements

What to Do

Step 1: Design your instrument

Design a short survey or interview guide. Write questions that help you understand user needs, current search behavior, common difficulties, and trust in AI answers.

Step 2: Pilot and revise

Check for vague, leading, or double-barreled wording. Run a small pilot with 1–2 people. Revise based on feedback.

Step 3: Collect data

Use your finalized instrument to gather data from your target users.

Step 4: Analyze findings and define requirements

Identify repeated question types, common frustrations, trust-related concerns, and patterns across users. Define functional requirements and trust/usability requirements.

Output
  • A short survey draft or interview guide
  • A brief note explaining what the questions are meant to find out
  • A short note describing any revisions you made and why
  • A summary of findings: common question types, major pain points, trust requirements
  • Initial system requirements (both functional and trust-related)

What to Learn

  • How to generate multiple design ideas before committing to one
  • How to turn user needs and requirements into interface ideas
  • How to create a simple prototype before building the full system
  • How to represent the main interaction flow clearly
  • How to design for usability, clarity, and trust

Design Principles to Apply

SimplicityKeep the interface focused; avoid unnecessary complexity.
AffordanceMake interactive elements look and behave as users expect.
PerceptibilityMake important information (sources, confidence cues) easy to notice.
ConsistencyUse consistent layout, wording, and interaction patterns throughout.
ConstraintsGuide users toward valid actions and prevent errors.

What to Do

Create a simple prototype based on your needfinding results and requirements. Design a basic interface that allows users to:

  • Enter a question
  • View an answer
  • View information that supports trust in the answer
  • Understand what happens when no good answer is found

Trust-supporting information may include: handbook sources, relevant excerpts, uncertainty/confidence cues, update information, and suggestions for what to do next.

Fidelity guidance: You do not need to write code. Paper sketches are fine for early exploration. Digital mockups (Figma, Balsamiq, etc.) are recommended for sharing and testing. The goal is to make your design testable, not polished.
Output
  • A low-fidelity prototype or interactive mockup (not a coded application)
  • A short explanation of the main interaction flow
  • A brief note explaining how the design reflects your user requirements

What to Learn

  • How to test a prototype with users
  • How to observe whether users can complete realistic tasks
  • How to identify confusion, trust issues, and usability problems
  • How to collect evidence for improvement
  • How to use the think-aloud protocol to capture user reasoning during tasks

What to Do

Step 1: Define your evaluation goals

Decide what you want to learn: Can users find answers? Do they understand the source? Do they know what to do when the system is uncertain? Do they trust the result?

Step 2: Choose your evaluation method

Use task-based usability testing. Ask participants to think aloud — verbalize what they are thinking, expecting, and noticing. Optionally, supplement with a brief heuristic evaluation.

Step 3: Prepare realistic tasks

Create tasks based on real handbook use cases: finding the attendance policy, checking a dress code rule, deciding whether an answer is trustworthy.

Step 4: Test with users

Recruit 3–5 users from your target group. Record: task completion, time, confusion points, trust cue understanding, think-aloud comments.

Take notes during each session. A simple table with columns for task, success, time, and comments works well. Write a short summary after each session while details are fresh.

Step 5: Identify issues

Summarize the main usability and trust-related problems that appeared during testing.

Output
  • A short evaluation summary
  • A list of usability problems
  • A list of trust-related issues
  • A list of improvements that should be prioritized

  • How to improve a design based on evidence
  • How to prioritize the most important problems
  • How to revise a prototype step by step

What to Do

  • Review the problems found during evaluation
  • Choose a few high-priority issues to fix
  • Revise the prototype
  • Test the updated version again with 2–3 users, if possible

↓

Quick Start (6 steps)

1. Install Node.js and Python
2. Install ChromaDB: pip install chromadb
3. Start ChromaDB: chroma run --path ./chroma_data
4. In a new terminal: cd starter-code && npm install && cp .env.example .env → edit .env with your OpenAI key
5. Load handbook: node ingest.js handbook.txt
6. Start server: npm start → open http://localhost:3000
This is a shell, not a finished product. Students must make their own decisions about page design, chunk strategy, citation display, "I don't know" handling, error states, and deployment.

Task 6 — RAG & ChromaDB Concepts

Understand the AI system you are about to build. This is the knowledge foundation for Stage 2.

What is RAG?

RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Instead of relying only on a language model's memory, RAG first retrieves relevant documents from a knowledge base, then uses them to generate a grounded answer.

1. RetrieveSearch a document database (e.g. the school handbook) for chunks most relevant to the user's question.
2. AugmentPass the retrieved text to the language model as context, alongside the user's question.
3. GenerateThe model produces an answer grounded in the retrieved text, not just its general training data.

Why RAG Fits the School Handbook

  • Handbook information is specific and changes each year — a general LLM would not know it.
  • RAG keeps answers grounded in real school documents, reducing hallucination.
  • RAG naturally supports citation: we know exactly which document chunk the answer came from.

What is ChromaDB?

ChromaDB is an open-source vector database. It stores text chunks as numerical embeddings so you can find semantically similar content quickly.

EmbeddingEach handbook chunk is converted into a vector (a list of numbers) that captures its meaning.
Similarity SearchWhen a user asks a question, the question is also embedded. ChromaDB finds the chunks whose vectors are closest — most relevant.

Why Metadata Matters

Each chunk stored in ChromaDB can carry metadata such as section title, page number, and document name. This metadata powers the citation and source display in the final product.

The Full Pipeline

Handbook PDF/DOCX
↓
Parse text → Split into chunks → Add metadata (section, page)
↓
Embed each chunk (OpenAI Embeddings) → Store in ChromaDB
↓
User asks a question → Embed the question
↓
ChromaDB finds the most similar chunks
↓
Send chunks + question to GPT → Generate answer with citations
↓
Display answer + source to user

What to Do This Week

  • Read through the RAG concepts above until you can explain them in your own words
  • Set up your development environment (Node.js, Python, ChromaDB)
  • Clone and run the starter code
  • Ingest a sample handbook and ask questions through the app
  • Observe: Which questions get good answers? Which don't? Why?
Output
  • A running local copy of the starter code
  • A short written explanation of what RAG is and why it fits this project
  • Notes on what you observed when testing with the sample handbook

Task 7 — MVP Build

Turn your Stage 1 prototype into a working web application.

Week 6 — Frontend

Translate your tested prototype design into real HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

What to build
  • A question input page based on your prototype design
  • An answer display area showing the AI response

  • What did you learn from needfinding and data collection?

  • How does your RAG system work?
