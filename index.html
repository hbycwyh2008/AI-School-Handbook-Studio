Project Overview

Students design and build an AI-powered school handbook experience for a real user group such as students, parents, teachers, or new families.

Project TitleAI School Handbook
Driving QuestionHow can we design an AI-powered school handbook experience that helps users find school information in a trustworthy and usable way?
Core BlendHCI / UX process, AI product thinking, RAG understanding, prototype testing, iteration, and MVP development.

Required Project Elements

  • a clearly defined target user
  • user research
  • a focused problem statement
  • a prototype
  • an AI answer flow
  • citation or source visibility
  • at least one round of testing
  • at least one round of iteration
Teaching principle: Give students the foundation, not the final answer.

Teacher Role

The Teacher Is
  • the studio lead
  • the framework provider
  • the concept explainer
  • the feedback and checkpoint reviewer
  • the scope manager
The Teacher Is Not
  • the main builder
  • the main designer
  • the person making all decisions
  • the person writing the whole product for students
Important: You may provide shared structure, AI concepts, and a minimal technical starter, but students must still own the user, the problem, the design choices, the testing decisions, the iteration decisions, and the final explanation of the system.

What the Teacher Provides

1. Project BriefA shared project framing document with the project theme, required stages, deliverables, scope expectations, and final presentation expectations.
2. Minimal Starter WebpageA lightweight starter UI such as page layout, header, input field, button, answer area, and citation placeholder. It should be a shell, not a completed product.
3. Minimal Technical StarterA basic example of frontend-to-backend flow, one sample AI response structure, and a sample citation/source structure.
4. Shared Concept LessonsMini-lessons on what RAG is, why school handbook questions fit RAG, what ChromaDB is, what retrieval does, and why citation and trust matter.
5. Process StructureA clear workflow: Research → Define → Prototype → Test → Build → Iterate → Present.
6. Checkpoints and RubricA review system that lets the teacher manage quality and scope without taking project ownership away from students.

Student Master Checklist

A single master list students can use across the whole project.

Checkpoint Forms

Use these as review gates. Students move forward only after each checkpoint is approved.

Checkpoint 1 — Research Approval Form
  • Target User: Who is your main target user? Why does this user need help with the school handbook?
  • Research Focus: What problem do you think this user may have? What do you want to learn from interviews?
  • Interview Plan: Who will you interview? How many people? Why did you choose them?
  • Interview Questions: Write at least 5 open-ended questions.
  • Teacher Check: clear user, realistic interview plan, open questions, no feature-jumping.
Checkpoint 2 — Problem Definition Approval Form
  • Research Summary: 3 to 5 important things learned from users.
  • Key Quotes: at least 3 real quotes or observations.
  • Pain Points: top 3 pain points.
  • Main Problem: the one problem the team will solve first.
  • Persona: user type, role, wants, frustrations, and why the problem matters.
  • Challenge Statement: How might we... ?
Checkpoint 3 — Concept Approval Form
  • List at least 2 possible solution ideas.
  • Explain which concept you chose and why.
  • Describe the main user flow.
  • Describe the citation / trust plan.
  • Define what is included and not included in MVP.
Checkpoint 4 — Prototype Approval Form
  • Required screens: homepage, answer/results page, citation/source page or section.
  • Explain homepage rationale, first action, source checking, uncertain answers, and biggest uncertainty.
  • Prepare 2 to 3 prototype test tasks.
Checkpoint 5 — Prototype Testing Review Form
  • List test participants and tasks.
  • Record what users did well and where they struggled.
  • Record whether users noticed or understood citations.
  • Identify top 3 usability problems.
  • State what will change before building.
Checkpoint 6 — MVP Scope Approval Form
  • State the one main thing the MVP must do well.
  • List core features.
  • List features intentionally excluded.
  • Explain frontend, backend, answer flow, and citation flow.
  • Explain what the system retrieves and why RAG is useful.
Checkpoint 7 — Live Testing Review Form
  • Link or location of tested MVP.
  • List test participants and tasks.
  • For each task, note success, hesitation, answer understanding, and citation use.
  • Explain whether users trusted the answer and why.
  • List main problems and next improvements.
Checkpoint 8 — Final Reflection Form
  • Summarize the final product.
  • Explain user, problem, and why it matters.
  • Explain design decisions and how research affected them.
  • Explain why citation/source matters.
  • Explain why RAG is useful here.
  • Reflect on what changed after testing and what should improve next.

Teacher Rubric

Use a 4-level scale: 4 = Strong, 3 = Proficient, 2 = Developing, 1 = Beginning.

Category 4 — Strong 3 — Proficient 2 — Developing 1 — Beginning
User Research Clear target user, real interview evidence, strong context understanding, research clearly influences design. Reasonably clear user, basic interviews, some useful findings, research mostly connects to design. Vague user group, limited research, general findings, weak design connection. No clear user group, little or no research, design mostly based on guessing.
Problem Definition Clear and focused problem, evidence-based pain points, meaningful tasks, strong scope control. Mostly clear problem, reasonable pain points and tasks, manageable scope. Broad or vague problem, weak distinction between needs and features, scope may be too wide. Problem is unclear, needs and features are confused, scope is unfocused.
HCI / UX Design Quality Design strongly supports task, source visibility is useful, trust and uncertainty handled thoughtfully. Design supports the main task, source visibility exists and is usable, trust is considered. Design is somewhat confusing, source visibility is weak or poorly integrated. Design does not support user tasks well, source visibility is missing or unclear.
Prototype, Testing, and Iteration Prototype is testable, findings are specific, major usability issues identified, revisions are meaningful. Prototype is usable, testing completed, main issues identified, some revision completed. Prototype is incomplete or hard to test, testing is limited, findings are general. Little or no testing, no meaningful iteration.
AI / RAG Understanding Clearly explains why RAG fits, understands retrieve → answer → citation flow, connects AI decisions to user needs. Basic understanding of RAG, can explain the system flow, knows citation is important. Surface-level understanding, partial explanation of system flow. Cannot clearly explain why RAG is used or how answer/source relate.
Technical Implementation MVP works reliably for the main task, answer flow and source flow both function. MVP mostly works, core features are present, source support is mostly functional. MVP is partially working, core flow is unstable or incomplete. MVP does not work well, major pieces are missing.
Scope Control and Product Thinking Solves one important problem well, clear must-have vs nice-to-have distinction, strong prioritization. Reasonably focused, some prioritization visible. Scope is a bit too broad, priorities are unclear. Scope is unrealistic and too scattered.
Reflection and Communication Explains the project clearly, justifies decisions well, reflects honestly, shows strong ownership. Explains the project clearly enough, gives reasonable reasons, includes some reflection. Describes the project but struggles to explain why, reflection is shallow. Cannot clearly explain decisions, reflection is weak or missing.

Starter Webpage

A minimal static shell that students can revise instead of building basic UI from scratch.

What is included:
  • A page layout with header, main area, and footer
  • A text input field for the user question
  • A submit button
  • An answer display area
  • A citation/source placeholder section
What is NOT included:
  • Completed visual design or branding
  • Backend logic or API connections
  • Real AI answer generation
  • Final styling decisions

Students are expected to take this shell, customize it for their target user, apply their own design decisions, and connect it to the AI answer flow they build during the MVP phase.

Sample AI Response Format

A clear answer + citation/source structure students can build around.

Example Response Structure

User Question

"What time does the school library close on Fridays?"

AI Answer

"The school library closes at 4:30 PM on Fridays during the regular school year."

Source / Citation

Source: Student Handbook 2025–2026, Section 5.2 — Library Hours
Page: 23
Confidence: High — direct match found in handbook text.

Key Elements in Every Response

AnswerA direct, concise response to the user's question.
SourceThe specific handbook section, page, or document the answer came from.
Confidence SignalAn indication of how closely the retrieved content matched the question.
Design principle: Users should always be able to verify where the answer came from and judge whether to trust it.

Mini-Lessons on RAG and ChromaDB

Shared concept teaching so students do not have to guess the system logic.

What is RAG?

RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Instead of relying only on a language model's memory, RAG first retrieves relevant documents from a knowledge base, then uses them to generate a grounded answer.

1. RetrieveSearch a document database (e.g. the school handbook) for chunks of text most relevant to the user's question.
2. AugmentPass the retrieved text to the language model as context, alongside the user's question.
3. GenerateThe model produces an answer grounded in the retrieved text, not just its general training data.

Why RAG Fits the School Handbook

  • Handbook information is specific and changes each year — a general LLM would not know it.
  • RAG keeps answers grounded in real school documents, reducing hallucination.
  • RAG naturally supports citation: we know exactly which document chunk the answer came from.

What is ChromaDB?

ChromaDB is an open-source vector database. It stores text chunks as numerical embeddings so you can find semantically similar content quickly.

EmbeddingEach handbook chunk is converted into a vector (a list of numbers) that captures its meaning.
Similarity SearchWhen a user asks a question, the question is also embedded. ChromaDB finds the chunks whose vectors are closest — meaning most relevant.

Why Metadata Matters

Each chunk stored in ChromaDB can carry metadata such as section title, page number, and document name. This metadata is what powers the citation and source display in the final product.

Key takeaway: RAG = Retrieve relevant chunks → Feed them to the AI → Generate a grounded, citable answer.
